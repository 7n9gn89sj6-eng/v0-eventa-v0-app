# Black Sea — Notes

The Black Sea, known to the Greeks as the Euxine Sea, has been part of the Hellenic world for over two and a half millennia. Greek colonies were established along its coasts beginning in the seventh century BCE, creating a network of settlements that maintained connections with the Greek mainland while developing their own local character.

These colonies were not isolated outposts but thriving communities engaged in trade, agriculture, and cultural exchange. Cities like Sinope, Trapezus (Trabzon), and Olbia became important centres of Greek culture and commerce. The Black Sea region provided grain, fish, and other resources to the Greek world, while Greek language, architecture, and ways of living took root along its shores.

The region's history is complex, shaped by the movement of many peoples and the rise and fall of various empires. Greek communities persisted through Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman rule, maintaining their language and cultural identity even as political control changed hands. Today, Greek-speaking populations and Greek cultural influence can still be found in parts of the Black Sea region, particularly in Turkey and Ukraine.

When this site refers to the Black Sea, it does so as a cultural geography rather than a simple geographic feature. The term acknowledges the region's long connection to the Hellenic world, recognising that Greek movement and settlement created networks of cultural exchange that have endured over time. The journeys and reflections gathered here extend to this region not because of modern borders but because of historical and ongoing connections.

The Black Sea represents the eastern extent of the Hellenic world's reach, just as the Gates of Hercules marks the western boundary. Together, these points frame the wider geography of Greek movement and settlement, from the western Mediterranean to the Black Sea, that this site explores.
