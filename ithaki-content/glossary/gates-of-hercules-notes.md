# Gates of Hercules — Notes

The Gates of Hercules, more commonly known in English as the Pillars of Hercules, refers to the Strait of Gibraltar, the narrow passage where the Mediterranean Sea meets the Atlantic Ocean. In ancient Greek geography and mythology, this point marked the western boundary of the known world and the limit beyond which lay the unknown.

The name comes from the myth of Heracles (Hercules in Latin), who was said to have set up pillars or markers at this point during his travels. The two promontories on either side of the strait—Gibraltar in Europe and Jebel Musa in North Africa—were identified as these pillars. For ancient Greeks, passing through the Gates of Hercules meant leaving the familiar Mediterranean world and entering the vast, unknown Atlantic.

In practical terms, the Gates of Hercules marked the western extent of Greek exploration and settlement. Greek colonies were established along the Mediterranean coasts of Spain and France, and Greek traders ventured into the Atlantic. The strait itself became a crucial point of passage for trade and movement between the Mediterranean and the wider world.

The concept of the Gates of Hercules persisted long after antiquity. Medieval and early modern European explorers saw themselves as following in the footsteps of those who had passed through the gates, and the phrase "plus ultra" (further beyond) became associated with the idea of venturing past the ancient limits.

In the context of this site, the Gates of Hercules represents the western boundary of the Hellenic world's reach. The journeys and reflections gathered here extend from this point eastward to the Black Sea, following the routes of Greek movement and settlement throughout the Mediterranean. The term acknowledges that the Hellenic world is not confined to the Aegean but encompasses the full extent of Greek cultural influence and movement.
