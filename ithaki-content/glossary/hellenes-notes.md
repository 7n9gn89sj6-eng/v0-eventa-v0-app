# Hellenes — Notes

Hellenes is the name Greeks use for themselves. It has been in continuous use since antiquity, appearing in texts from the classical period onward. The term refers to people who identify with Greek language, culture, and history, regardless of where they live or their political citizenship.

In ancient times, "Hellenes" distinguished Greek speakers from other peoples. The criteria for being a Hellene were cultural and linguistic rather than strictly ethnic or political. Greek colonies throughout the Mediterranean and Black Sea regions were populated by Hellenes, even though they were far from the Greek mainland.

Today, the term remains in use in Greek and is increasingly adopted in English when speaking about Greeks in their own terms. It emphasises cultural connection and continuity over political boundaries. A person may be a Hellene whether they live in Athens, Melbourne, or New York, whether they hold Greek citizenship or not.

The distinction between "Hellenes" and "Greeks" is subtle but meaningful. "Greeks" is the English term derived from Latin, while "Hellenes" is how Greeks name themselves. Using "Hellenes" respects Greek self-understanding and acknowledges that identity is not simply a matter of nationality but of language, culture, and connection to a shared history.

In the context of this site, "Hellenes" appears when speaking about living people and communities, recognising that the Hellenic world is not only a matter of geography or history but of ongoing human experience and cultural practice.
